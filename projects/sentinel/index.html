Cory Chang


Signaling the Sentinel

A piece on the impact of cybersecurity tools on workplace surveillance.



Current Cultural Importance

 During the coronavirus pandemic, a large number of corporations, primarily in tech, sent their employees home, ushering in the era of remote work. For many employees, has granted them greater flexibility than ever before, with 54% of employees desiring to continue remote working even after the pandemic, according to research by the Pew Research Center.

 Unfortunately, the rise of remote work also means a corresponding increase in cyberattacks, as Jaber and Fritsch explain that “computers at home lack the same security features as those in company networks,” just as “cybercrime is increasing in frequency and severity.” The combination of these two factors have pushed IT departments to install increasingly invasive tools onto work devices to reduce risk of cyberattacks harming the company.

 As these tools are installed, however, companies quickly discover the power of the collected data at their fingertips. Even the most well-intentioned company, intending simply to reduce their risk of cyberattacks, can quickly find themselves in “function creep.” According to Ball in the journal Labor History, “function creep” is where the surveillance devices “yield more information than intended,” and as a result, management is tempted to “extend monitoring pratice[s] without consulting employees” (93). These tools, originally intended simply to secure the devices, can easily begin monitoring the employees as well.

 The powerful collection tools installed on devices can be exploited to monitor individual movements, not just of computer processes, but nearly every action taken by the human on the other side of the screen. If not treated with caution, function creep can mean some of the most invasive tools are now being used to surveil every worker.

 The potential of workplace monitoring is found in the Microsoft Sentinel suite of products. Advertised as a “birds-eye view across the enterprise,” it certainly does an excellent job of protecting a company against security threats and attacks. The choice to name the product “Sentinel” is also evocative of its true abilities as a piece of software that will always be present and watching.

 Much more rare, however, are the discussions of potential abuse from the collected datasets.

 To provide an idea of the level of detail that even the most basic Sentinel workspace can provide, we can look at a sample query. Similar to that of querying a search engine for information, a simple request for the data can generate info about a device (and thus employee’s) movements.

 | where Computer == "timh-pc.contoso.local"
 | where EventID == "12" or EventID == "42" or EventID == "1" or EventID == "1074"
 | where TimeGenerated > todatetime('2022-11-29T00:00:00.0000000Z') 
 | where TimeGenerated < todatetime('2022-11-30T00:00:00.0000000Z')

 This second query goes deeper, depicting every single application that the device has started, with the data from the first query interpolated. Through this, an employer can track not only when an employee was working, but precisely which applications the employee was using.

 Even in the most sparse Sentinel collections, the power of this telemetry data is clear. It can be used to track deeply specific movements of every employee, enabling workplace surveillance in a deeply damaging way.

 Clearly, the world is currently navigating difficult changes and dealing with tensions as the workplace is entirely upended in ways that have not been seen since the Industrial Revolution. People are working from home, from halfway across the world, from nearly anywhere, and employers are trying to adapt quickly. Management’s desire to keep a closer eye on its remote workforce, coupled with powerful surveillance tools, has led to a unique environment where employees actions are carefully watched, oftentimes even closer than in the office.



Prefigurations

 Well before modern technological tools existed, the desire to conduct workplace surveillance was always there. Primarily, the purpose of this form of surveillance is to shift the “dynamics of power in the workplace in favor of firms” and regulate the behavior of employees, according to Zickuhr, a labor policy analyst at the Washington Center for Equitable Growth. Before the high-tech big-data analysis commonly performed today, simpler methods of surveillance also existed and continued to surveil employees.

 Towards the end of the 1930s, the La Follette Civil Liberties Committee was formed in the United States Senate, with a focus on preventing union-busting during that period of time. Through the investigation, the Committee “revealed espionage to be a ‘common, almost universal, practice’ in American industry,” according to Auerbach in The Journal of American History. Furthermore, according to White of The Smithsonian, some of the first instances of wiretapping occurred during this time, as “a widespread and viciously effective tool for corporate management to root out union activity.” These instances of wiretapping are some of the first instances of technological surveillance of employees.

 Going back further in time, to the Industrial Revolution, it is clear that workplace surveillance was even present there, in the regulated way that employees were treated and watched. A clear example of this is Henry Ford’s Sociology Department, created in 1914. Adopted in each of the manufacturing plants quickly, the goal of the Sociology Department was to “increase efficiency in production, and therefore increase profits by reducing the rate of turnover in the labor force,” according to Hooker in the Journal of American Culture. Specifically, Ford had set out a series of standards for each of his workers, including ones that extended into their personal lives, and expected each worker to uphold them to retain their job, known as the “Lee Reforms.” The Sociology Department was tasked with the work to ensure that workers upheld such standards, going as far as to make unannounced calls on employees and their neighbors to collect information [on their] lifestyles” (Manokha). In the modern context, it seems like an extreme invasion of privacy, yet the surveillance technologies in the remote workplace today are not so dissimilar. Despite not having a person physically inspect your house, the power of digital surveillance tools like Microsoft Sentinel are derived from the same concept, for the employer to ensure that workplace standards are kept and maintained. For example, one of the key expectations of the “Lee Reforms” was to have “good habits,” a quite subjective term that left much of the discretion to the investigator (Hooker). Similarly, tools such as Sentinel provide a large dataset full of data points that can be interpreted in whichever manner management wants to, while providing deep and broad insight into the habits of each employee.



 Tools such as Microsoft Sentinel, while a product of the digital age, are certainly not a new concept, as clearly seen from the widespread workplace surveillance conducted prior to the evolution of high-tech products. From wiretapping for union-busting purposes to an entire department dedicated to surveilling the culture of its employees, the modern set of tools can similarly extract as much information, while being far more discreet, under the guise of computer security.



Social Implications

 At its most fundamental, the issue of workplace surveillance is one that is highly related to the right for individuals to have privacy. While the employer certainly has the right to conduct monitoring to comply with regulations and to reduce the risk of cyberattacks, there is still no clarity for when an employer goes from acceptable monitoring levels to intruding upon an employee's personal rights. There are, however, a couple key factors to consider, such as how the data is being kept and the effectiveness of the technology.

 In the case of Microsoft Sentinel, there are interesting questions surrounding the data being collected. The modern technology landscape is driven by mass data collection without “consent-driven data practices,” frequently justified by the need to create “systems of profitable computational ‘intelligence,’” according to Crawford in Atlas of AI (95). Unfortunately, Microsoft Sentinel is no exception, collecting and “analyzing trillions of signals daily” to train the “built-in machine learning and knowledge” (Microsoft). This dataset comes directly from the actions of every user in the system, and each of the pieces of data seen in the demonstration above are directly fed into this system. Importantly, there is no evidence that employees have the right to opt into or out of this system, effectively making the training of AI a compulsory part of the workplace.



 Ultimately, these tools can often be considered an invasion of privacy as well. According to Zickuhr of the Washington Center for Equitable Growth, the rise of surveillance is a clear violation of privacy, “by giving employers direct or indirect access to sensitive or protected information, from health data to personal details such as religion, family structure, or sexuality” (13).  As seen in the short clip of a potential message between two coworkers, the risk of surveillance means that colleagues who might be friends discussing personal matters inadvertently share those same personal matters to management. That can be very dangerous when employees have information they prefer not to share with management, but end up doing so because they used work communciations.

 Even if an employer is not explicitly trying to collect this information, the mass amounts of harvested data from employee devices means that much of this data is collected and analyzed anyways, oftentimes fed into the same AI machinery mentioned above. With the rise of surveillance also comes the invasion of privacy, and without careful protections for workers’ rights through legislation, it can be difficult for workers to fight back.

 Even disregarding the concerns around the right to privacy and training AI, these surveillance tools frequently do not even work. Interestingly, Ball contends that these surveillance technologies may not even be effective, frequently “producing the behaviors it was designed to prevent” (93). When tools such as Sentinel are deployed far beyond the intended scope of securing devices against cyberattacks, the result is actually a stronger pushback against those surveillance techniques, with employees subverting the surveillance in any way possible. In fact, according to Ball, studies have demonstrated that “intense surveillance increases resistance, sabotage and non-compliance with management” (94). Ultimately, the fact that deploying tools for surveillance purposes does not work should highlight the importance of using software carefully.

 The takeaway of these surveillance technologies is not that they should be rid of altogether, but that they need to be managed carefully and with great worker input. Ultimately, when deployed properly and used in-scope of expectations, they are extremely effective in protecting the company and employees from cyberattacks. But, if these tools are used carelessly, they can feed into a terrible system that enables invasions of privacy.



The goal of this piece was to provide a glimpse into the power that surveillance technologies have in the digital age. While cybersecurity professionals are typically careful about the utilization of the data being collected, the sheer amount of telemetry collected means that the data can be used powerfully in other contexts.

I chose Microsoft Sentinel as the SIEM/SOAR product to focus on because of the wide-ranging data analysis tools within its suite. Many might disagree, but KQL queries are super intuitive for me to understand, and it made writing the queries to pull data much easier. As a part of Microsoft, it also has the greatest brand recognition, providing an understanding that this is not some niche field, but a product used by nearly every corporation in one form or another.

Because of the sensitivity of the data, live information obviously could not be used to populate this demo. As a result, the data is primarily generated using KQL queries run on the demo Log Analytics agent provided by Microsoft here. The KQL queries chosen were a result of what I believe are a likely starting point for employers, who after seeing the hoards of information, decide they want to start surveilling employees. They are definitely very simple queries, but already provide a rich fountain of information about the actions of every single employee.

A quick Python parser was written to format the data seen on each right subpanel. Unfortunately, I cannot publish the parser used at this time. JavaScript was used in the on-page animations.
